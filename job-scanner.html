Automated Job Scanner

Julianne Toh · March 2026

Built with n8n, Claude API, Gmail

What it does

An automated workflow that scans job listing sites daily, filters for roles matching my target profile (AI product management, fraud/scam prevention, government tech, policy), and sends a Gmail summary of roles worth considering. It replaces the manual process of checking multiple job boards daily and trying to remember what's new.

Why I built it

My job search targets a specific intersection of skills (fraud prevention, AI product management, government experience) that doesn't map neatly to standard job categories. Searching manually meant either using broad keywords and drowning in irrelevant results, or using narrow keywords and missing roles that were a strong fit but described differently. I needed a system that could apply judgment about relevance, not just keyword matching.

How it works

An n8n workflow triggers daily and scrapes job listings from target sources. The listings are sent to Claude API with context about my background, target roles, and what makes a role a good or bad fit. Claude evaluates each listing against my criteria and returns a ranked summary with a brief explanation of why each role is worth considering (or not). The output is delivered via Gmail so I can click through directly to listings each morning. A separate workflow monitors my Gmail for interview updates and sends alerts when application status changes.

What I learned

The hardest part was not the automation; it was translating my own judgment about job relevance into instructions an AI could follow. I initially used keyword filters, but that created the same precision-recall tradeoff I dealt with at Amazon: too specific and I missed good roles described with different language, too broad and I got noise. Switching from keyword rules to giving Claude my full context and letting it apply judgment (more like how I'd actually evaluate a listing myself) improved the results significantly. This is the same lesson from my Amazon work: the bottleneck in automation is encoding human judgment, not building the technology.

I originally planned to auto-update my Google Sheets job tracker from Gmail updates, but descoped it when I realized the matching logic (figuring out which email update corresponded to which row and what stage to update) was surprisingly complex relative to the value. Updating manually took two minutes a day. The automation would have taken hours to build and debug for marginal time savings. Recognizing when not to automate is as important as knowing how to automate.
