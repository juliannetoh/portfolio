Strengthening Singapore's Scam Intelligence Architecture

Practical ideas for accelerating detection and enforcement

Julianne Toh · February 2026

This is an independent analysis, not a policy recommendation. It draws on publicly available information about Singapore's scam landscape and my professional experience in fraud detection. Some of these ideas may already be in progress. Gaps in my understanding are acknowledged in the final section.

Where I'm coming from

I spent a decade in the Singapore Police Force (SPF), including three years in the Commercial Affairs Department (CAD) investigating scams, payment fraud and cross-border financial crime. At CAD, I set up processes and methodology for working with DBS Bank's structured digital transaction data: proposing the shift from paper bank statements for strategic projects, designing fund-flow mapping methodology, and training ASCom investigators on the new approach. I also served as the intelligence lead in SPF's collaboration with Carousell on e-commerce fraud, defining the data requirements, obtaining platform-level data including device identifiers and chat logs, and conducting the analysis that resolved a long-running scam series.

I then spent four years at Amazon as a Global Program Manager for financial fraud, owning the product strategy for buyer-seller collusion detection. I increased enforcement automation from 5% to 55% through launching automated enforcement actions and ML systems including graph-based cluster detection, human-in-the-loop workflows, and large language models. This delivered over US$7.6 million in annual bad debt reduction. A key lesson: the top 20 bad actors accounted for roughly 50% of collusion losses. High-precision enforcement on high-confidence targets consistently outperformed broad approaches.

The problems

Singapore lost S$456.4 million to scams in the first half of 2025. Case numbers dropped 26% year-on-year, a real sign that existing measures are working, but the average loss per victim jumped significantly, and total losses still climbed 16.4%. The scams that remain are harder to detect and more damaging per case. I see four structural gaps:

1. Speed of data access. When a scam is reported, investigators rely on largely manual and bilateral channels to request bank transaction records. By the time fund flows are mapped, money has often moved through multiple hops into cryptocurrency, cash, or gold.

2. Authorised push payments are invisible to traditional detection. 78.8% of scam transactions involve victims voluntarily making the transfer. The Shared Responsibility Framework addresses phishing scams but doesn't yet cover this broader category of authorised push payment (APP) fraud. There's no systematic detection layer that helps banks identify when a voluntary payment is likely scam-induced before it executes.

3. Syndicate enforcement is still largely manual. Scam operations are networks, not individual actors. The ability to automatically detect that Account A at Bank X, Account B at Bank Y, and Account C at Bank Z are part of the same ring, and act on all three simultaneously, would be a step-change in enforcement speed.

4. Government Official Impersonation Scams (GOIS) have gone physical. GOIS nearly tripled in H1 2025 (+199.2%). Scammers now direct victims to purchase gold bars and hand over valuables to physical mules. At least 384 such cases caused S$20.2 million in losses. Digital-only interventions can't fully address this.

What I'd build: a four-layer scam intelligence architecture

These proposals form a single integrated system where each layer feeds the next: faster data access enables better analytics, which powers smarter intervention, which generates outcome data that improves the models.

This is an ambitious vision describing an ideal end state. Getting there would require significant technical investment from banks and government, sustained multi-agency coordination, possible legislative changes to enable digital warrant authorization, and careful privacy protections at every stage. What follows is a direction, not a detailed implementation plan.

Layer 1: Structured, digital, near-real-time data access

Solves: Speed of data access (Problem 1)

The foundation is replacing manual, bilateral data requests between police and banks with a structured API layer. When an investigator needs transaction records for a reported scam account, the request would be submitted digitally through a secure system, routed to the relevant bank's API, and returned as structured data ready for analysis. Warrant authorization would be digitized, with every request immutably logged for accountability. The legal threshold for data access wouldn't change. The manual bottleneck of emails and format reconciliation would be removed.

This should extend beyond the seven D-SIBs to include fintechs (Revolut, Wise, GrabPay), licensed payment service providers, and cryptocurrency exchanges operating in Singapore. SPF's Crypto Tracing Team already conducts blockchain analysis, but integrating that capability into the same analytical pipeline as fiat fund flows would give investigators a unified view of how money moves across both systems.

The technical investment from each bank would be substantial. But there's precedent. COSMIC required similar integration across six banks, moving from legislative amendments to the FSMA in mid-2023 to operational launch in April 2024. A pilot with DBS, ASCom's existing FRONTIER partner, would be the practical starting point.

Real-time data access is the aspiration, but some latency is realistic. At Amazon, not all our ML model inputs were real-time. I prioritized a few choice signals that were real-time while others had a two-day data lag. A similar approach could work here. Confirmed scam account identifiers shared in near-real-time, while richer transaction datasets follow with a short delay. Even a few hours is dramatically faster than the current situation.

Layer 2: Automated clustering and confidence scoring

Solves: Syndicate-level enforcement (Problem 3) and precision targeting

Once transaction data flows in through Layer 1, we need automated analysis that identifies connected scam accounts, clusters them into likely syndicate groups, and assigns risk scores so investigators can prioritize.

At Amazon, I worked with a data scientist to build a cluster-based detection model for buyer-seller collusion. The existing system relied on deterministic signals like linked devices or shared bank accounts, which bad actors learned to circumvent. We shifted to behavioral signals: shared listing patterns, overlapping buyer networks, transaction timing. The cluster model identified connected fraud networks that individual-account analysis would miss entirely.

The same principle applies to scam enforcement. A mule account at Bank A might look unremarkable in isolation. But when its transaction patterns cluster with accounts at Bank B and Bank C showing similar inbound/outbound flows, timing patterns, and connections to known victims, the picture changes. The analytical layer would ingest transaction data from Layer 1, run clustering algorithms to identify likely networks, assign confidence scores, and surface results to investigators with clear explanations of why each cluster was flagged.

Human validation is essential at this stage. Each flagged account should come with a clear rationale so investigators can make informed decisions and explain enforcement actions to stakeholders. The system should use risk-based, adaptive scoring rather than hard-coded thresholds. And it needs safeguards against cascading enforcement to legitimate businesses that happen to receive one transaction from a mule account.

If government agencies already have graph analysis or network analysis capabilities for a different purpose, it could dramatically reduce the investment required. At Amazon, we didn't build from scratch. Another team had built a base ML model and my data scientist layered on collusion-specific signals to customize it.

Layer 3: Alerting, notification, and bidirectional intelligence sharing

Solves: APP fraud detection (Problem 2), faster enforcement across institutions, and closing the intelligence gap

The real value of the first two layers is the intelligence they produce. Given a confirmed scam account, the system surfaces the network of likely connected mule accounts that manual investigation wouldn't find, as well as likely active victims. The question for Layer 3 is how that intelligence reaches the banks that need to act on it, and how intelligence from banks and platforms flows back to law enforcement.

Outbound: Police to banks and platforms. The practical starting point is a standardized, secure notification system. When the analytics layer identifies high-confidence targets, the relevant banks receive structured alerts. Each bank then executes using its own internal processes, just as they do today through co-location. The difference is that the notification arrives in minutes, and it includes accounts the system surfaced through ML clustering that wouldn't have been found through manual investigation.

Enforcement should still be graduated. For the highest-confidence mule accounts, banks apply immediate holds. For likely victims, banks apply cooling-off periods or enhanced monitoring on outgoing payments. For lower-confidence flags, accounts enter an investigation queue. Human enforcement decisions remain critical. Investigators authorize enforcement tiers based on the system's risk assessment and their own judgment.

An important design question is who coordinates this data exchange. Banks may be more willing to participate in a system where intelligence flows through MAS as their regulator, rather than directly to law enforcement. MAS already has established frameworks for receiving sensitive financial data, and banks are accustomed to regulatory reporting. Routing certain data flows through MAS could significantly increase institutional buy-in. The trade-off is speed: adding a regulatory intermediary could introduce latency in precisely the scenarios where rapid action matters most. A hybrid approach may be practical, with MAS as the coordination layer for broader intelligence sharing and lower-urgency data exchange, while preserving direct ASCom-bank channels for the most time-critical interventions like active scam disruptions.

Precision requirements should scale with the severity of the action. Account freezes or temporary holds need extremely low false positive rates (1-2% or less) because the cost of freezing a legitimate account is high. Pulling transaction records for analytical purposes is lower stakes and can tolerate wider nets. For high-impact actions, every enforcement notification should document the analytical basis clearly. For example: "rapid inbound transfers from multiple reported victims, pattern consistent with known mule network." This matters because no single bank sees the full picture. A mule account might look clean at Bank B if the suspicious activity is concentrated at Bank A. Clear documentation of the cross-institutional evidence protects against wrong actions and creates an audit trail for the feedback loop.

Inbound: Banks and platforms to police. The data flow shouldn't be one-way. Banks and digital platforms already run sophisticated AI fraud detection models that identify potentially suspicious accounts daily. However, there's no systematic mechanism for that intelligence to flow back to law enforcement at operational speed.

Layer 3 should create a structured channel for this. When a bank's fraud model flags an account exhibiting scam-like behavior, that signal should flow into the same analytical system that generates enforcement alerts. Similarly, when platforms detect accounts involved in scam operations, those signals should integrate with SPF's intelligence picture.

This bidirectional flow expands the intelligence base beyond victim reports, enables faster syndicate mapping across institutions, and creates a feedback loop that improves everyone's detection models. The Global Signal Exchange, which Singapore joined in September 2025, creates a precedent for scam signal sharing with tech companies. The next step is systematizing and accelerating that exchange.

The governance challenge is defining what threshold justifies sharing. The system needs risk-based tiers: high-confidence detections trigger automatic sharing, medium-confidence flags enter a review queue, low-confidence stays internal unless other signals corroborate.

Layer 4: Feedback and learning

Every hold or freeze produces an outcome that needs to be captured: confirmed mule, confirmed victim, false positive, inconclusive. Structured labelling of these outcomes is what turns enforcement actions into training data.

But labeling alone isn't enough. At Amazon, our data scientist built training datasets from confirmed enforcement actions, filtering out cases overturned on appeal to ensure the model learned from accurate decisions. Equally important were weekly reviews of the highest-loss cases that ML didn't catch, diagnosing whether the gap was a threshold issue, a missing signal, or an evolution in bad actor tactics. That diagnostic loop is what keeps a system adaptive rather than static. The same principle applies here, with an added dimension: regular multi-stakeholder reviews of system performance, false positive rates, and evolving scam tactics would build the institutional trust needed for sustained buy-in from banks, platforms, and law enforcement.

Addressing physical vectors: GOIS as an emerging gap

GOIS nearly tripled in H1 2025, with physical handovers of gold bars and cash now the dominant method. SPF and gold dealers are already responding. As recently as November 2025, BullionStar staff alerted police to a suspicious transaction, leading to arrests. But this is happening organically, case by case.

There's an opportunity to systematize this: an ASCom intervention hotline for gold dealers, merchant training on common GOIS indicators, and victim pattern analysis to predict likely targets for proactive outreach. This is Phase 2 work but worth flagging because the scam ecosystem is evolving beyond digital-only vectors.

Policy foundations and implementation path

Building on what exists

Singapore already has the building blocks. COSMIC proves that legal frameworks for structured bank data sharing on financial crime can be created. It took legislative amendments to the Financial Services and Markets Act, but it's now operational across six major banks. Project FRONTIER proves the operational model works: co-location of bank staff with ASCom reduced account-freezing turnaround from weeks to within a day, and ASCom recovered S$56.7 million in H1 2025. The challenge is extending these foundations to real-time, ML-enhanced scam response. COSMIC's scope is AML, not scam enforcement, so new legal authority or parallel infrastructure would be needed. But the precedent for how to build cross-institutional data sharing in Singapore's regulatory context already exists.

Critical policy enablers

Making this work likely requires navigating several policy and regulatory dependencies. Legal authorization for API access would need to extend the CPC provisions or FSM Act framework to authorize law enforcement access to bank transaction data through secure APIs, with clear judicial oversight and audit requirements. An algorithmic accountability framework would need to cover dispute resolution for frozen accounts, liability allocation when the model is wrong, and transparency requirements that balance explainability with preventing bad actors from gaming the system. Enforcement authority for APP fraud would either need to extend the SRF to cover authorised push payment fraud or create a new regulatory basis for banks to act on predictive intelligence. And bidirectional intelligence sharing would require establishing legal basis and protocols for banks and platforms to share suspicious account intelligence with law enforcement, including risk thresholds, data protection compliance, and handling of international platform data.

Proof of concept: what ships in 90 days

The fastest path to demonstrating value starts with Layer 2 (Automated clustering). Clustering and confidence scoring can run on ASCom's existing historical case data without requiring any bank to build new infrastructure. Run the system in shadow mode: it scores accounts and generates recommendations, but only investigators see them. No enforcement actions fire automatically. Compare the model's output against investigator judgment on the same cases. If the system identifies significantly more connected mule accounts than manual investigation would catch, that proves the analytical value before asking banks to invest in API integration.

Once accuracy is validated, a Layer 1 (API) pilot with one bank becomes a much easier sell. DBS is the natural starting point given the existing FRONTIER relationship and ScamShield data-sharing pilots. Start with a narrow use case: confirmed scam accounts from reported cases only, structured digital requests replacing the current manual process. Measure time from request to data delivery. If that drops from days to hours, expand scope. Layer by layer, the system proves itself before requiring larger commitments.

This mirrors how I rolled out collusion detection automation at Amazon. Shadow mode first, validate accuracy, introduce enforcement gradually starting with highest-confidence cases, tighten thresholds over time as stakeholders build trust in the system.

What I don't know

I'm writing from the outside, and there's a lot I can't see. Some of these ideas may already be in progress. I don't have visibility into ScamShield's technical roadmap, SATIS's expanding capabilities, ASCom's current data infrastructure, or ongoing MAS initiatives.

What I do know: the gap between scam reporting and enforcement action is where money disappears. Precision-focused automation outperforms broad approaches. And the most impactful solutions come from working directly with product teams, coordinating across government and industry, and building practical frameworks through iteration rather than designing them in isolation. These are ideas meant to contribute to the broader conversation about Singapore's scam response architecture. I welcome discussion, critique, and collaboration.
